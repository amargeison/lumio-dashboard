# Demo Notes — Game Standards & the Carney Review

Briefing note for anyone demoing the women's-football Game Standards
module. Written 2026-04-30 alongside Sprint 3.5 (Carney accuracy patch).

This is a content correctness briefing, not a structural overview of
the module. For the structural overview see the GameStandardsView
component itself.

## What "the Carney Review" actually is

There are two documents to know about:

1. **Raising the Bar: Reframing the Opportunity in Women's Football**
   — Karen Carney MBE, July 2023. 128 pages. Independent review
   commissioned by DCMS. 10 strategic recommendations.
   gov.uk/government/publications/raising-the-bar-reframing-the-opportunity-in-womens-football

2. **Government Response to Independent Review** — DCMS, December
   2023, Command Paper CP 965, ISBN 978-1-5286-4530-0. The formal
   government response with implementation actions across all 10
   recommendations.

When someone says "Carney" or "the Carney Review," they mean the 2023
report, not the broader Lionesses brand. Karen Carney is the author,
not the review's subject.

## The 10 recommendations — the way Carney actually wrote them

The headline mistake to avoid in demos is paraphrasing the 10 like
they're abstract principles. Carney wrote them as long, specific
sentences directed at named bodies. The verbatim titles in the
module reflect that. Examples worth memorising:

- Rec 1: aimed at "the new entity tasked with running elite women's
  football" — i.e. **WPLL** (Women's Professional Leagues Limited).
  WPLL took over running the WSL and Championship from the FA in
  the **2024-25** season.
- Rec 3: the operational core of the review. Seven sub-recommendations
  covering the gulf between WSL and Championship, medical and mental
  health, training facilities, parental package, union representation,
  duty of care, and career transition.
- Rec 6: six sub-recommendations on fans and standards. The
  operationally significant one is **6.4** — extending SGSA licensing
  to all WSL grounds. This is a stadium-safety/capex topic, not a
  fan-experience one.
- Rec 7 is a **government action**, not a club action. It's about
  girls' equal access to school sport. The card shows national
  progress and notes the club's contribution flows via Foundation /
  Community work.

## NewCo vs WPLL terminology

The 2023 report uses **"NewCo"** as a placeholder for the new
operating entity. The actual entity that was set up is **WPLL**.

When demoing:

- In **paraphrased explainer text**, always say "WPLL." The module
  does this consistently.
- In **verbatim quoted sub-principles**, the module preserves
  "NewCo" as it appears in the source document. Don't apologise for
  this — it's the correct way to quote a 2023 source.

## Realistic demo statuses — the 7/10 philosophy

The aggregate score in the top-right is **"7 / 10 strategic
recommendations on track"**. This is intentional.

The module's demo philosophy: showcase that the dashboard surfaces
work-in-progress with traceability, not perfection. If every
recommendation showed compliant, the demo would be making the
opposite point — that compliance is a finished project rather than
an ongoing operating discipline.

The 3 not-on-track demo recommendations are:

- **Rec 2** — talent pathway funding. Strategic partner decision
  pending WPLL Year 1 review.
- **Rec 3** — the operational heart. Three sub-recs compliant
  (3.3 facilities, 3.5 union, 3.6 duty of care), four in active
  remediation (3.1 contact-time gulf, 3.2 medical/mental, 3.4
  parental, 3.7 career transition).
- **Rec 8** — national grassroots facilities investment. Lionesses
  Futures Fund delivery is proceeding nationally; club's local
  contribution is in motion but not yet at full scale.

## WPLL Minimum Standards tab — what's actual licence criteria

The 2023-24 FA training facility licence has **eight published
minimum standards**. The Training Facilities category in the WPLL
tab uses these verbatim:

1. Full-size grass pitch ≥100m × 64m, OR 3G artificial pitch
2. Separate goalkeeper training area
3. Adequate pitch lighting
4. High-quality performance gym (cardio, free weights, plyo)
5. Changing rooms for players AND staff
6. Adequate medical facilities (defibs, rehab) per medical lead
7. Meeting room for players
8. Adequate office space, parking, storage for staff

These are not made up. If a stakeholder asks "where do these come
from?" the answer is "thefa.com licence criteria, 2023-24 version,
the same set every WSL and Championship club is audited against."

The other five WPLL categories map to specific Carney sub-recs:

- Salary & Contact Time → Carney 3.1
- Medical & S&C → Carney 3.2
- Maternity & Parental → Carney 3.4
- Union Rep & Duty of Care → Carney 3.5 + 3.6
- Stadium Safety → Carney 6.4

Each category card shows the Carney link in the header so the
audience can trace any metric back to a specific recommendation.

## Equal Access Audit — two axes

The Carney Review repeatedly emphasises two parity gaps. The Equal
Access tab now has a toggle for both:

- **Axis 1 — Women's vs Men's First Team.** This is what most people
  think "equal access" means. The 10-row table shows the gaps:
  medical hours, S&C, scouting, video analysis, etc.
- **Axis 2 — WSL vs Championship.** This is the Carney 3.1 priority.
  Most cells are at parity (FA standards apply universally), but
  contact time and salary floor still differ structurally.

When demoing, pivot between the two axes. A stakeholder who's only
aware of "women vs men" will be reframed by the WSL/Championship
view: a lot of the gulf the review was worried about is
**within the women's pyramid**, not between men and women.

2. ACL Prevention Programme (3.2)
3. Mental Health Provision (3.2)

5. Independent Welfare Officer (3.6)

7. **Career Transition Support (3.7)** — added Sprint 3.5
8. **Post-Retirement Medical Access (3.2)** — added Sprint 3.5

Both new cards are deliberately demo-tagged as **partial**, not
green. They reflect the genuine state of the industry: most clubs
are scoping these, very few have them fully operationalised.

## Strategic Investment tab — the demo's killer feature

Every line of the multi-year remediation plan is **tagged to a
specific Carney sub-recommendation**. This is the differentiator
to lead with.

The demo line: *"Here's our 3-year remediation plan against
specific Carney sub-recs, with traceable receipts. Every pound
investment links to a specific recommendation. Here's the proof
trail."*

The plan totals **£2.4m over 3 years**:

- Y1 (2026-27) — £0.4m: Carney 3.4 (childcare), 3.7 (career
  transition), 3.2 (post-retirement medical)
- Y2 (2027-28) — £0.8m: Carney 3.3 (training ground), 3.1
  (Championship contact time → 20 hrs/wk)
- Y3 (2028-29) — £1.2m: Carney 6.3 (main stadium hosting),
  9 (Premier League Stadium Fund-supported upgrade)

## Common stakeholder questions and the right answers

**Q. Why isn't every recommendation green?**
*Because compliance is an operating discipline, not a finished
project. The dashboard exists to surface where work is still in
flight with traceability, not to claim perfection.*

**Q. What's the difference between WPLL and the FA?**
*The FA still governs the women's pyramid. WPLL operates tiers 1
and 2 (WSL, Championship) since 2024-25. The FA holds a "golden
share" governance position; WPLL has independent decision-making
on operations.*

**Q. Is Article 48 going to be revoked?**
*Not yet. Article 48 of the UEFA Statutes prohibits live broadcasts
on Saturday 14:45-17:15 in member countries. Government has called
it "one viable option" for women's football specifically.
Discussion is ongoing.*

**Q. How is the £302m grassroots figure split?**
*Lionesses Futures Fund alone: £30m (£25m government + £5m FA),
delivering 30 new pitches with gold-standard provision for women
and girls. The remainder is across the broader UK-wide grassroots
facilities envelope.*

**Q. Why does the Equal Access tab show structural gaps as "0"?**
*Because some "gaps" aren't gaps in provision quality — they're
different operating models. SGSA licensing is required for WSL
grounds (post-2024 secondary legislation); Championship clubs use
SGSA-supported self-regulation. Both are compliant — they just
operate under different regulatory regimes.*
